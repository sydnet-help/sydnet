Summary

Social theory

There are two main branches of social science: applied social science and basic social science. Applied social science is generally done in government departments, corporate marketing or research departments, and non-government organisations, with the aim of helping institutions and individuals make better decisions. Basic social science is genderally done in universities, and published in scholarly journals and books. Its goal is the development of generalised knowledge - which means knowledge which provides explanations across more than just the situation, example, or cases being studied.

One of the main ways that basic social science develops, discusses, and formalises generalised knowledge is through the development and testing of various social theories. These theories are complex abstractions. Theories differ in the fundamental conceptual categories they use (e.g. “the individual”, “classes”, “genders”, “nations”) and the relationships between these categories (e.g. conflict, cooperation, interdependence, exploitation).

When social scientists use the word theory they generally mean one of two very different types of abstractions. At the highest level of abstraction, social scientists use theories to mean frameworks, such as Marxism, functionalism, or rational choice theory. These frameworks are much like religious or political beliefs - they are different ways of thinking about the world, which people are often strongly attached to, and which help them make sense of the world, BUT they are generally hard to convincingly test and definitively prove right or wrong (or likely or unlikely).

At a more mundaine and practical level, social scientists use theories to mean explanations. Explanations provide an account of how or why some outcome occurs. Explanations are generally complex - with many interconnected parts. Explanations are almost always in a contestation with other explanations for the same outcome. For example, in the following research question, we can see two competing explanatory frameworks (individual effort and social class): “Is individual effort or social class more important in determining life outcomes?” What distingishes explanations from frameworks is that (1) explanations tend to focus on an outcome - they are are trying to explain an outcome; and (2) explanations are generally able to be tested (though generally only indirectly, and partially tested - since explanations are complex, with many interconnected and unseen parts).

2.1 Units of analysis: The ‘things’ studied

In qualitative analysis, as with quantitative analysis, we (1) closely look at our primary sources; and (2) search for patterns.

The basic method of searching for patterns in qualitative data is called thematic analysis or thematic coding. This involves classifying different parts of our data into meaningful categories - categories which we call codes or themes.

2.2 Variables: Characteristics of the ‘things’.

2.2.1 Dependent variable: The outcome. The effect. The bloody nose.

Generally we start a coding process by engaging in open coding, where we develop our own codes inductively, as we first examine the texts or images we are analysing.

2.2.2 Independent variable: The cause. The predictor. The punch.

The second stage of coding involves recoding our data using a strict set of rules. This process is called axial coding.

At the end of axial coding, our data has been formally coded.

At this stage, we generally review the results and confirm that the theory or argument we are making is actually correct.
